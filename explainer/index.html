Ozni · gunshot detection

Can a phone tell a gunshot from a firework?

Ozni turns ordinary smartphones into a gunshot-detection network for community sites. Here's the whole idea — and the one hard problem at its heart — with no jargon, and real sound (turn it up 🔊). Scroll.

01 — THE RAW MATERIAL

Sound is just a wiggle in the air

Every sound is air pressure wobbling back and forth. A microphone feels those wobbles and writes them down as a wiggly line — about 48,000 dots every second.

Two things describe any wiggle: how tall it is (loudness) and how fast it repeats (pitch). Drag the sliders — and tap to hear the tone change.

02 — THE FIRST CLUE

A gunshot is a punch, not a song

A voice rises and falls gently. A gunshot is a near-instant slam: silence, then a wall of sound in under a thousandth of a second, then gone. That violently fast "attack" is the first thing a detector looks for.

A firecracker string is different again — a rattle of many little pops. Watch the three draw themselves, then hear each one.

03 — THE HIDDEN COLOR

Every sound has a color

Pull any sound apart and it's a stack of pitches at once, like a chord. Low pitches are boom and bass; high pitches are bright and sharp.

A spectrogram paints that: time runs left→right, pitch runs bottom→top, and brightness is how much energy sits there. A gunshot shows up as a bright vertical slash — a burst of every pitch at the same instant.

04 — THE WHOLE PROBLEM

A big firework is a gunshot's twin

Here's the question everything hinges on. A single large firework — a Brazilian bomba — is also one loud, broadband slam. Put them side by side and, to a phone, they look almost identical.

This is the kill-or-cure question: if a phone genuinely can't separate these two, the product can't work. So far, the honest answer is "this is hard."

They look like twins — now listen. The only real tell is a faint low boom in the firework, and the next two sections show why even that often gets erased.
05 — WHERE INFO IS LOST (1)

Too loud, and the mic gives up

Every microphone has a ceiling. A close gunshot is far over it, so the tops of the wiggle get sliced flat — "clipping." The sharp punch we needed to recognize is blunted into a flat-topped mush. Turn it up and watch — and hear — it break.

06 — WHERE INFO IS LOST (2)

Distance and an open window dull everything

Sound loses its high pitches as it travels, and a wall or window strips them faster. From across the street, a sharp crack arrives as a soft thud, smeared with echo.

A match heard in a living room, near the TV, window open, is exactly this: a real sound, softened and blurred by the room and the open window, buried in street noise. That's the world Ozni actually has to work in.

07 — HOW OZNI LISTENS

Two steps: a bouncer, then a judge

Listening to everything with a heavy AI model would drain the battery in an hour. So Ozni uses a cheap bouncer first: it sleeps through the hum of the world and only wakes on a sudden bang.

Then the judge — a trained model — studies that one bang and asks: gunshot, or firework? Only a confident gunshot raises an alarm. Watch a sound travel through.

08 — THE REAL TENSION

The crying-wolf dial

Here's the trade nobody escapes. Make the judge twitchy and it catches every real shot — but screams at every firework. Make it calm and it never false-alarms — but might sleep through the real thing.

The whole job is one sweet spot: catch ~9 of 10 real shots while staying silent through a fireworks-filled night. Find it.

09 — WHAT'S LEFT

We have the "no." We need the "yes."

Teaching the judge needs examples of both. Examples of what isn't a gunshot are everywhere — every match, every New Year's, every firework night fills the jar.

What we don't have yet is the real thing: actual gunshots, recorded on a phone, at honest distances. A few hundred of those flips this from "unknown" to "measurable." That's the next step — a day at a range.
